Lanternhouse

The light that protects us was never meant to keep the dark out.

Story Bible & Game Systems

Story Bible Premise Tone Acts Classes Quests Factions Conflict Theme Opening Chapters | Overworld Battle Equipment Economy Crafting Fishing Housing Companions Progression Reference

Lanternhouse is a classic retro adventure about ordinary lighthouse workers trying to keep a failing beacon network alive across a fog-haunted island chain, only to discover that the light protecting their towns also hides old debts, abandoned people, and truths no one wants restored.

01 Core Premise

The island chain of Mournlight Sound depends on an old network of beacons called the Lantern Line. Each lighthouse protects a route: ships at sea, roads through woods, bridges over marshes, mountain passes, town borders, old ruins where the fog gathers.

When a beacon goes dark, that region becomes isolated. Monsters appear. Roads shift. People panic. Trade stops. Rumors spread.

The Lanternhouse party starts as a group of young workers, tenants, apprentices, and misfits assigned to the least glamorous beacon station in the chain.

Then the main Keeper disappears.

No prophecy. No royal command. No goddess. No crystal choosing them.
Just an empty duty roster and a storm coming in.

Long ago, the islanders built the beacons. The official story says they guide ships through the reefs. The real story is that the Lantern Line seals away something beneath the island chain. The beacons are failing because someone — or something — is breaking the old pattern.

Story Pillar

Lanternhouse is about maintenance, responsibility, and survival — not prophecy. The world does not need heroes. It needs someone to carry oil through monster-filled roads, repair broken lenses, reopen paths between towns, bargain with scared locals, keep watch at night, and make hard calls when resources are low.

The party becomes important because they keep showing up, not because fate picked them.

03 Story Arc

Act I — The Local Light

  • Fix the Lanternhouse beacon.
  • Learn the fog is not natural.
  • Discover the old Keeper left behind a coded map.
  • Visit Brindlewick and meet key NPCs.
  • Clear the first forest/cave area.
  • Relight Beacon 2.

Act II — The Broken Line

  • Travel to other towns and islands.
  • Each beacon has a local problem — bandits, forest encroachment, a collapsed chapel, a missing lens, a noble family's control, a tide-locked tower.
  • The party learns the Lantern Line was built after an ancient pact.

Act III — The Thing Beneath

  • The fog is trying to free an old sea-bound entity.
  • But the entity may not be pure evil.
  • The Lantern Line may also be imprisoning lost souls, old gods, or the island's original people.
  • Endgame choice: restore the old seal, break it and risk change, or rebuild the Lantern Line into something new.

Act I Turn

The party finds the missing Keeper, Mara Venn, at Beacon Three. She is alive. She did not disappear because she was attacked.

She left because she discovered Beacon Three was not failing — it was being deliberately overfed with oil to keep something nearby suppressed.

When the party asks who is doing it, Mara says:

"All of us. For years. We just stopped asking what the light was for."

No villain reveal. Expands the mystery. Makes the beacon system morally complicated.

  • Dockmaster refuses to release oil
  • Refugees claim their town needs it more

07 Factions

The Keepers' Guild

Public face

Disciplined, practical, trusted. The official maintainers of the Lantern Line.

Reality

Understaffed, secretive, full of missing records, terrified of admitting ignorance.

Conflict: They may obstruct the party to avoid panic. They are not evil. They are overwhelmed.

The Harbor Compact

Public face

Keep trade alive, feed towns, control shipping. The economic backbone.

Reality

Hoard oil and supplies. Decide which towns are "worth" saving. Profit from scarcity.

Conflict: Selfish, but not cartoonishly evil. If shipping fails, everyone starves.

The Grey Chapel

Public face

Comfort the grieving, keep burial rites, tend the sick. Local faith around mourning and storms.

Reality

Believes some beacons should stay dark. Knows old sea rites. May protect forbidden history.

Conflict: They oppose relighting certain towers — and they may be right.

The Unlit

Public face

Raiders, thieves, road threats. Refugees from towns abandoned by the Lantern Line.

Reality

People who were left outside the light. Angry at the towns that survived.

Conflict: They steal oil and lenses. They also expose the moral cost of the beacon system.

08 The No-Clear-Villain Rule

Instead of one obvious bad guy, the conflict comes from competing pressures. The player should often think: "I get why they're doing this, but we still have to deal with it."

  • 🌫️ The fog is dangerous, but may be natural or wounded, not evil.
  • 🔦 The old Keepers lied to prevent panic.
  • 🏛️ Town leaders hoard supplies trying to protect their people.
  • ⚔️ Bandits steal oil, but some are refugees from darkened towns.
  • ⛪ Religious groups oppose relighting beacons — but they may know old truths.
  • 🐙 Sea things attack ships — but maybe ships have been trespassing over sealed places.
  • 🏮 The Lantern Line saves towns, but may also be damaging the world in some hidden way.

09 Central Theme

Light is not goodness. Darkness is not evil.

🕯️ Light Means

  • Safety
  • Order
  • Roads
  • Authority
  • Visibility
  • Control

🌑 Darkness Means

  • Danger
  • Uncertainty
  • Secrets
  • Grief
  • Forgotten people
  • Things pushed away

The party's job is not simply "destroy darkness." It is to decide: what deserves to be kept lit, and what has been hidden too long?

The Central Mystery

The Lantern Line is failing in ways that do not make sense. Some beacons are extinguished. Others burn blue. Some attract monsters. Others show impossible places through their lenses. A few light themselves when no one is near. Townsfolk share dreams.

The Lantern Line was not built all at once. It was assembled over generations from lighthouse engineering, folk magic, emergency repairs, lies told by town councils, bargains with the sea, and old ruins nobody fully understands.

Nobody alive truly understands the whole system.

The world is breaking because everyone inherited a machine they depend on but no longer understand.

10 Opening Sequence

The game begins in Brindlewick. The player party lives in the Lanternhouse, a drafty beacon station doubling as a boarding house for apprentices, workers, and people with nowhere better to stay. The old Keeper, Mara Venn, has been gone for two days.

During a hard storm, the main beacon fails.

At first, it feels like a maintenance problem. Then:

  • The harbor bell rings with no one pulling it
  • A fishing boat crashes into rocks it should have avoided
  • The road to the woods disappears under fog
  • A supply runner arrives injured from a beacon farther inland
  • The town council refuses to send help — every able adult is needed at the docks

The party is handed a ring of keys, an oil ledger, and a bad map.

Get the Lanternhouse light burning again before morning.
No one calls them heroes. Most people expect them to fail.

12 Overworld & Exploration

The main exploration mode is a 40×40 tile grid built from an ASCII map. Each character in the map array represents a terrain type, rendered as 32×32 pixel tiles using the Pipoya RPG World Tileset.

Movement & Controls

Four-direction grid movement at 0.15 seconds per tile. Supports WASD, arrow keys, and gamepad. Sprinting is available on some terrain, which reduces the encounter step divisor by 2 (minimum 3), meaning more frequent encounters while running.

Fog of War

The map starts shrouded. Tiles within a 3-tile radius of the player are revealed as they move. Lit beacons permanently reveal a 6-tile radius around their position. The fog overlay dynamically adjusts alpha based on distance to the nearest lit beacon.

Day/Night Cycle

A full cycle lasts 300 seconds (5 minutes). Four phases tint the screen:

  • Night — deep blue overlay
  • Dawn — warm amber tint
  • Day — clear (no tint)
  • Dusk — orange-pink fade

NPCs change position by phase. Wandering radius shrinks at night.

Weather

Rain rolls in every 40–120 seconds (25% chance per check) and lasts 20–60 seconds.

Gameplay effect: +8% miss chance in battle during rain.

Rain darkens the day/night overlay and adds visual atmosphere.

Random Encounters

Encounters are checked every N steps based on terrain type:

Forest1 in 6 steps
Mountain1 in 5 steps
Grassland1 in 8 steps
Beach1 in 10 steps
Post-Seal1 in 5 steps

! tiles are guaranteed encounters (one-time, replaced with grass after). Cleared encounters have a 30% chance to respawn every 120 seconds. There is a 1-in-10 surprise chance at the start of any battle. Fog in a Bottle blocks all encounters for 60 seconds.

The Five Beacons

Scattered across the map are five beacon towers. Lighting each one:

  • Reveals a 6-tile radius permanently
  • Grants +5 Keepers Guild reputation
  • Increases all shop prices by 5% (the beacon modifier)
  • Opens gated routes (Hill Overlook beacon unlocks the eastern pass)

Lighting all five grants +25 Keepers Guild reputation and cracks the cave seal, unlocking deeper exploration. The beacon network is central to both the story and the economy.

Gathering

Walking over herb-bearing tiles triggers a gathering check: 40% base + 10% per alchemy skill bonus, with a chance at bonus yields. Materials follow the same pattern using the tinkering skill. Different terrain types yield different herbs and materials.

13 Battle System

FF1-style turn-based combat using a state machine. The round flows through phases: command → resolution → between, cycling until all enemies are defeated or the party flees/falls.

Turn Order

Each combatant rolls AGI + random(0, 20). All participants (party + enemies) are sorted by this roll, descending. Ties go to the party. Turns resolve one at a time in this order.

Commands

1 — Fight

Physical attack. Damage = ATK - DEF + rand(0,2), minimum 1. Critical hit on roll ≥ 0.90 (10% chance): 2× damage with gold flash.

2 — Guard

Doubles effective defense for the round. The character takes roughly half damage from all incoming attacks until their next turn.

3 — Magic

Cast a spell from an unlocked spell level. Each level has a fixed number of charges that recharge at inns. Spells target a single enemy or ally.

4 — Item

Use a Tonic (heals 20 HP to lowest-HP ally) or Ether (restores full magic charges to lowest-MP caster).

5 — Run

Escape chance: 20% + (leader AGI - avg enemy AGI) × 6%, clamped 5–85%. Failure = free enemy attack at -2 DEF penalty. Smoke Bomb guarantees escape.

Tab — Skip

Pass this character’s turn. They do nothing and take no special stance.

Damage & Accuracy

  • Base damage: max(1, ATK - DEF + rand(0,2))
  • Critical: 10% chance, 2× damage, gold screen flash, shake effect
  • Miss: 5% base + 30% if blind + 8% if raining
  • Guard: Doubles effective DEF for the round
  • Loyalty modifier: ≥80 loyalty: +2 STR/DEF; ≤20: -2 STR/DEF

Victory Rewards

Defeat Penalty

Leveling Up

XP needed for next level scales at next_xp × 1.4 per level. Maximum level is 40. Beyond that, diminishing returns grant only 1–3 bonus to a random stat. Each class has its own level-up stat gain ranges (Fighters gain 3–6 HP, Thieves gain 0–1 AGI, etc.).

Effective stats combine base stats + weapon ATK + armor DEF + loyalty bonuses. At 80+ loyalty, companions get a 1.1× multiplier to STR and DEF. Equipping a new item automatically swaps the old one back to the bag.

Inventory Tabs

The inventory screen has four tabs, cycled with Tab key: Consumables (tonics, ethers, crafted items), Weapons (ATK + equipped indicator), Armor (DEF + slot tag + equipped indicator), Trade Goods (base value + zone modifiers). Press Enter on an item to equip it to a chosen party member.

16 Crafting & Skills

Two crafting disciplines, both driven by a use-based skill progression system. Skills improve by doing — each use increments a counter, and crossing thresholds unlocks new tiers.

🌿 Alchemy

Gather herbs from terrain tiles. Craft potions and elixirs at an alchemy workbench (town or home kitchen).

8 recipes across 4 skill levels:

  • Novice: Minor Tonic (15 HP)
  • Adept: Tonic (30 HP), Antidote
  • Expert: Greater Tonic (60 HP), Ether Brew
  • Master: Strength Elixir, Defense Elixir, Panacea
🔧 Tinkering

Scavenge materials from terrain tiles. Craft tools and trade goods at a workbench (home).

7 recipes across 4 skill levels:

  • Novice: Repair Kit
  • Adept: Lantern Lens, Simple Lockpick
  • Expert: Beacon Lens, Oil Lantern
  • Master: Master Repair Kit, Trap Kit

Cooking

Fish with cooking value can be cooked at a home kitchen (requires upgrade) or with the tavern keeper. Cooking heals all alive party members by the fish’s HP value (8–200 HP) and tracks cooking skill progress. Cooked fish are consumed in the process.

Quest Structure

Act I — The Local Light (6 quests)

Relight the Lanternhouse beacon, find the missing Keeper, secure oil supplies, light the first beacons, explore the cave, and confront the Mournlight Shade.

Act II — The Broken Line (9 quests)

Travel to other towns, light remaining beacons, navigate faction politics, and complete personal quests for your companions (Kael, Lyra, Brok, Selene).

Act III — The Thing Beneath (1 quest + endgame)

The final descent. Face what the Lantern Line imprisons and make the endgame choice.

Companion Quests

Four member-specific quests unlock in Act II, each requiring the companion to be in your active party with loyalty ≥ 50–70:

Quest Rewards

Gold rewards range from 50 to 1,000 copper. XP rewards range from 5 to 80. The final quest grants 80 XP and 1,000 copper.

Auto-Save & Manual Save

The game auto-saves every 60 seconds to a single slot (user://saves/save_slot1.json). Press F5 for manual save, F7 to load. Saves store 35+ data fields including all party state, equipment, overworld position, quest flags, faction reputations, and skill use counts. The save format includes version tracking for backward compatibility.
